SHIPBUILDING & STRATEGIC INDUSTRIES

Japan was once the world's undisputed shipbuilding leader with over 35% global market share, but competition from China and South Korea eroded that to just around 11% today. The response has been aggressive.

The government issued a formal roadmap to double annual ship output to 18 million gross tons by 2035 and recover a 20% global market share. To do this, the roadmap calls for consolidating domestic shipbuilding companies into one to three groups by 2028 — a massive structural consolidation. The biggest move so far: Imabari Shipbuilding agreed to acquire a controlling 60% stake in Japan Marine United (JMU), a transaction aimed at strengthening Japan's position against Chinese and South Korean competitors.

On funding, the Shipbuilders Association of Japan plans to invest approximately $2.3 billion into the domestic industry, while inviting public sector contributions, to reach a total of $6.5 billion by 2035.

The innovation angle is equally important. Strategic initiatives such as the Smart Ship Project, Zero Emission Ship Project, and Maritime DX Consortium highlight Japan's focus on digital transformation and green vessels — essentially moving away from cheap commodity ships toward high-value, tech-intensive, eco-friendly builds.

Japan is also shifting its order mix. As higher-value vessel segments including tankers, gas carriers, and RoRos absorbed a larger share of shipyard capacity in 2025, Japan's bulk carrier orders declined sharply — a deliberate pivot to better margins.

GREEN TECHNOLOGY & RENEWABLES

This is Japan's biggest long-term structural bet. Japan's green technology and sustainability market size reached $6.6 billion in 2025 and is expected to reach $45.8 billion by 2034, growing at a CAGR of nearly 24%.

The policy backbone is Japan's 7th Basic Energy Plan (approved February 2025). It prioritizes decarbonization of power generation, positioning renewable energy as a principal power source. The government has committed to reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 46% by FY2030 compared to FY2013 levels. To fund all of this, more than ¥150 trillion is expected to be invested in the decarbonization sector by both public and private sectors over the next ten years.

Key sub-sectors within green tech:

Solar — Japan's largest source of low-carbon electricity. In 2025, its share reached 10% of generation for the first time, nearly tripling in a decade. Japan remains the world's fourth largest solar generator.

Offshore wind — still largely untapped at 1.1% of the mix but a major target. Japan plans to install up to 10 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2030 and 30 to 45 GW by 2040, while slashing the cost to 8 to 9 yen per kilowatt hour by 2030 to 2035.

Hydrogen & ammonia — Japan aims to increase annual hydrogen consumption to up to 3 million tonnes by 2030 and 20 million tonnes by 2050, and wants to achieve 20% use of ammonia as a mixed combustion fuel at thermal power stations by 2030.

EVs — Japan aims to eliminate sales of new gasoline-powered vehicles by the mid-2030s and generate nearly $2 trillion a year in green growth by 2050.

THE CONVERGENCE

The two sectors are actually converging — Japan's shipbuilding push is itself deeply tied to green tech, as zero-emission vessels using hydrogen and ammonia fuel systems are the very ships Japan wants to specialize in building.
